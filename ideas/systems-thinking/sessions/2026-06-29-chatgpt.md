# Thinking Session — 2026-06-29

_Explored in ChatGPT (GPT-5.5). This file is history; it is never edited after it is written._

## Summary

Reframed process engineering as an application of universal systems thinking. The
session began with the question of how to learn process engineering and concluded
that the field is best understood not as workflow optimization but as the
application of systems thinking to operational problems — the same mental model
used in software architecture, logistics, healthcare, and any other domain where
inputs are transformed into outputs under constraints.

## Insights

- Process engineering is broader than optimization; optimization is one phase within
  the lifecycle of designing, operating, measuring, and improving systems.
- The primary object of study is the **business objective** — not the current process.
  Understanding purpose before analyzing the process prevents local optimization.
- A useful hierarchy for any system: business objective → success metrics → current
  process → constraints → bottlenecks → improvements → measurement & iteration.
- Process engineering and software system design share equivalent abstractions:
  objectives ↔ requirements, process flow ↔ architecture, bottlenecks ↔ system
  constraints, performance metrics ↔ non-functional requirements.
- Many optimization opportunities come from eliminating non-value-added activities:
  waiting, unnecessary approvals, duplicate work, redundant handoffs.
- Experts ask good questions because they have internalized recurring system
  structures — not because they memorized domain-specific questions.

## What changed from before

**Previously:** Process engineering was viewed primarily as optimizing workflows for
metrics like cost.

**Now:** Process engineering begins by understanding *why* the process exists, and
only then optimizes the system supporting that business objective. Optimization
without purpose risks improving the wrong subsystem.

**Previously:** System design and process engineering were considered analogous.

**Now:** System design is one specialization of systems thinking; process engineering
is another. Both share nearly identical reasoning patterns around modeling systems,
constraints, flows, bottlenecks, and feedback loops.

**Invalidated assumption:** Optimization should begin by understanding the current
process. The corrected view: business objectives come first, so the right
process — or subsystem — is being optimized.

## Decisions made

- Use systems thinking as the primary framework rather than studying industries independently.
- Think of every process as a transformation of inputs into outputs under constraints.
- Build intuition by repeatedly modeling unfamiliar industries, not memorizing
  industry-specific knowledge.
- Leverage visual reasoning: construct layered system models rather than relying on
  textual descriptions.
